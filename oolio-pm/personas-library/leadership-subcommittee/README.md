# The Leadership Subcommittee

The commercial and executive review body of the Oolio Virtual Product Council. It assesses whether a product direction is strategically aligned, commercially valuable, financially viable, supportable, sellable, adoptable, and deliverable, before a major decision moves into PRD approval, steering review, or delivery planning.

This subcommittee does not debate waiter workflows, button placement, or screen hierarchy. Those belong to the Operator Council (`../uat-panel/`) and the Design Council (`../design-council/`). The Leadership Subcommittee answers the bigger question: should Oolio invest in this, what risk are we accepting, how should it be positioned, and what must be true before it moves forward.

Like the Design Council, these are lenses, not user personas. Each one is an executive or commercial point of view we test a decision against, written as a function, not as a named individual. Their job is to challenge a strong decision from their own angle, and to disagree with each other on purpose, so the decision is stress-tested before it is committed.

## The Behavioural Alchemist is convened here, not filed here

Roy, the Behavioural Alchemist (`../behavioural-alchemist.md`), is the other elevated role. He is not a member of this subcommittee, for the same reason the Chair is not: his remit spans all three panels. But this is the panel he visits most often, because pricing, packaging, positioning, and "is the reward valuable or merely expensive" are commercial questions. Convene him as a conditional seat when the decision touches his ground, using the row in the conditional table below. He argues perceived value against the CFO's cost discipline and against the Data and Analytics Lead's measurable evidence, and the panel is sharper for it.

## How to run a Leadership Subcommittee review

1. **Name the decision.** One sentence. What are we deciding, and what would change if we said yes.
2. **Convene the default nine, plus the Chair.** Then add the conditional lenses the decision actually touches, using the table above.
3. **Run each lens.** Read the file. Ask its challenge questions of the decision. Record where it passes and where it fails, and produce its review output.
4. **Surface the clashes.** The commercial lenses will pull against each other. The CRO wants to win the deal, the CFO wants the margin, Support wants the ticket load down, the CPO wants roadmap coherence. Write those disagreements down. Do not resolve them quietly.
5. **The Chair adjudicates.** The Chair weighs the trade-off, states the recommendation, the confidence level, the PRD changes, and whether it needs human approval. See `../product-council-chair.md`.

---

## The clashes are the point

This subcommittee is built to disagree. The CRO will argue a feature wins deals. The CFO will ask whether the return justifies the cost. Customer Success will warn that sold value and adopted value are not the same thing. Support will count the tickets it creates. The CPO will ask whether it strengthens the platform or fragments it. Engineering will price the complexity. Those tensions are healthy. A decision that survives them is a better decision. The Chair holds the floor of commercial discipline, the same way the Operator Council holds operational reality and the Design Council holds design quality.

## House rules for this folder

These sit on top of the house rules in `../personas.md` and the operating guide in `../CLAUDE.md`.

1. **Lenses, not job descriptions.** Each file is a point of view a decision is tested against. It is a tool, not an org chart entry.
2. **Functions, not named individuals.** These are roles (CPO, CFO, CRO). Do not attach a real person's name or invented quotes. The challenge questions are what the lens asks in a review.
3. **Keep the panel balanced.** If you add a lens, name the gap it fills and the lens it argues with. A panel that all agrees is useless.
4. **Default light, conditional on demand.** Do not run all seventeen lenses on every decision. Convene the nine defaults plus what the decision touches.
