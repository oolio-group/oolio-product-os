# Voice and writing rules

Authoritative ruleset for any content produced under this skill. Every artifact generated by the skill (one-pager, deck, playbooks, marketing pack) follows these rules, as does every interview question and every handover message.

If something doesn't match these rules, it gets rewritten before it leaves the skill. No exceptions for "this section is small" or "the user can fix it later".

---

## 1. Start with intent. Always.

Every piece of communication answers three things.

- What is this?
- Why does it matter?
- Who is it for?

If any of those are missing, it's not finished. This applies to a slide, a paragraph, a one-line caption, or a Slack message generated for handover. Bad writing describes activity. Good writing defines outcomes.

## 2. Structure beats style

Write in frames before paragraphs. The frames in this skill are:

For ideas: Problem, Context, Decision or recommendation, Outcome.
For product: Problem or Opportunity, Hypothesis or Solution, Success Metrics.
For decisions: Context, Options, Decision, Rationale.
For execution: Goal, Plan, Risks, Next steps.

If a section can't be broken into a clean frame, it's not understood properly. Re-think it before writing.

## 3. Language is direct and economical

No filler. No corporate theatre. No hedging.

Avoid: "I think", "we could potentially", "it might be worth considering", "however", "it's worth noting", "furthermore", "in conclusion", "leverage", "synergy", "utilise", "ascertain", "game-changing".

Use: "This solves...", "This fails because...", "Do this next...", "use", "find out".

You're not trying to sound smart. You're trying to remove interpretation risk.

## 4. Every sentence earns its place

Cut aggressively. A sentence stays if it does one of three things.

- Clarifies a decision.
- Defines a problem.
- Moves execution forward.

If it doesn't, delete it. Nobody has time to decode meaning.

## 5. Write like you're accountable for execution

Write as the person responsible for the outcome, not as a describer of ideas.

No vague ownership. No abstract recommendations. No "someone should". Every sentence implies action, ownership, or consequence.

## 6. Be specific or don't write it

Weak: "Improve performance", "Enhance experience".
Strong: "Reduce order entry time by 30% for FOH staff", "Increase AOV via modifier upsell prompts".

Specificity forces thinking. Thinking drives good product. If a number is unknown, mark it `[TBC]` and move on. Don't paper over the gap with adjectives.

## 7. Tone: calm authority

Controlled, confident, grounded in logic. No hype. No over-explaining. No emotional language unless it serves the point. You sound like someone who already did the work.

## 8. British English, consistently

Optimise, prioritise, colour, behaviour, organise, realise, analyse, summarise, centre, catalogue, favour. Hyphens correct on compound terms. No em dashes as a crutch, use commas or full stops.

This matters more than people think. It signals precision.

Industry-standard exceptions stay as-is: "artifact" (software sense), "program" (software sense). Brand and product names render as the brand spells them.

## 9. Product thinking is embedded in everything

Even when not explicitly talking about product, the mindset is there.

- What's the user problem?
- What's the business impact?
- What's the fastest path to value?

If it doesn't work on a busy Friday night service, it's irrelevant.

## 10. No buzzwords, no trend-chasing

If a concept can't be explained simply, it's either not understood or not valuable. Strip the buzzword and rewrite.

## 11. Write for smart people short on time

Assume the reader understands context quickly, doesn't want hand-holding, and will challenge weak logic.

- Front-load the important point.
- Don't bury conclusions.
- Don't repeat yourself.

## 12. Punctuation rules

- Replace em dashes with commas, full stops, or rewritten sentences. Never leave one in.
- No semicolons. Use full stops or "and" / "but".
- Remove mid-sentence ellipses unless they signal intentional hesitation.
- Limit parentheses. Blend the detail into the main sentence instead.
- Use colons sparingly. Don't announce lists with "Key points:" or similar.

## 13. Word and phrase swaps

| Don't | Do |
|---|---|
| utilise | use |
| ascertain | find out |
| however | but |
| furthermore | (delete) |
| in conclusion | (delete) |
| it's worth noting | (delete) |
| moreover | also |
| leverage (verb) | use |
| game-changing | (specific outcome instead) |
| synergy | (specific shared benefit instead) |
| robust | (specific quality instead) |
| seamless | (specific experience instead) |

## 14. The final test

If it sounds like it came from a marketing team, it's wrong.
If it sounds like it came from someone accountable for shipping product in a real venue environment, it's right.

When in doubt, read the sentence aloud. If it sounds like a press release, rewrite it.

---

## Worked example

**Before:** "The results, though preliminary, suggest success. However, it's worth noting limitations."

**After:** "Preliminary results suggest success. Limitations still need addressing."

**Before:** "We should look to leverage our existing infrastructure to deliver a more seamless customer experience and potentially drive enhanced engagement metrics."

**After:** "Use the existing ordering stack to remove three taps from kiosk checkout. Target 8% lift in completion rate."

**Before:** "Key points to consider when evaluating the rollout: timing, resourcing, dependencies."

**After:** "Rollout depends on three things. Timing, resourcing, dependencies."
